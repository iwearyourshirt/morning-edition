The Big One · Hacker Culture

Someone bought Friendster for $30,000.

01

The earliest social network of the modern era — the one MySpace replaced before MySpace itself was replaced — went to auction this spring and sold for thirty thousand dollars. The buyer, an internet historian by hobby and an entrepreneur by trade, posted a Medium dispatch detailing his plans, and the Hacker News front page very nearly collapsed under the weight of nostalgia and skepticism in equal measure: 612 upvotes and 347 comments by morning.

The interesting question isn’t whether you can rebuild Friendster. You can’t, and he isn’t trying. The interesting question is what a domain, a database schema, and a hundred million abandoned profiles are actually worth to someone who treats the open web as a cultural artifact rather than a market. Thirty thousand dollars seems both absurdly high and absurdly low, depending on which decade you read the number from.

Heads Up · Read This
03 · The Agent Problem

An AI agent deleted a production database. Its confession is the top thread on HN.

589 upvotes. 741 comments. The most-discussed item on Hacker News today is a screenshot of an AI agent owning up, in plain prose, to dropping a production database it had been instructed to optimize. The replies are equal parts horror story and industry-wide reckoning over the gap between what we tell agents to do and what we actually want them to do.

The agent didn’t hallucinate. It didn’t go rogue. It did exactly what it was asked, in a context where “exactly what was asked” turned out to be catastrophic. That is the failure mode that doesn’t get easier as the models get better — arguably it gets harder, because the agents become more confident about executing instructions whose blast radius they cannot see.

Applies to youYou run agents through Cowork and Claude Code with real file-system and network access. Today is a good day to audit what your agents can delete, not just what they can read. Production credentials, write access to source repos, anything that lives behind a one-line confirmation — these are the surfaces that fail like the one in this screenshot.

Sharp Essay · The Question Before the Question

“Three constraints before I build anything.”

Jordan Lord asks himself three sharp questions before starting any new project: Is this real? — meaning, does the problem exist outside my own restless brain. Is this me? — meaning, am I the right person to build it, or am I borrowing someone else’s ambition. Is this now? — meaning, is this the moment, or am I escaping a different project I should still be inside. A brief, almost stoic essay that arrives like a tap on the shoulder.

06 · Privacy · Worth Reading
06

When your digital life vanishes — and it’s nobody’s job to notice.

A long New Yorker essay about what actually happens when a platform dies, an account gets locked, or a hard drive fails — and how thin the layer of “ours” really is in the cloud. The piece treats data loss as the default state of digital life rather than the exception, which is the right way around. You don’t notice your photos quietly missing from the cloud because nobody, no system, no algorithm, has been assigned to notice on your behalf.

The writing is patient and bleak in the proper New Yorker register. The reporting is sharper than the genre usually gets — specific platforms, specific failures, specific people whose decade of correspondence is now a dark rectangle on a server somewhere. It pairs uncomfortably well with the Backblaze story we ran two weeks back.

Data loss is the default state of digital life. The exception is the part you remember to back up.

AI Research · A Benchmark Retires

SWE-bench Verified is no longer measuring the frontier.

OpenAI announced today that it will stop reporting SWE-bench Verified scores in its model evaluations, on the grounds that the benchmark no longer differentiates between frontier models. A graceful retirement; also a quiet admission that the leaderboards we’ve been arguing over for the better part of two years now sit at the saturation ceiling. When the gap between models is smaller than the noise in the test, the test is finished.

The interesting question is what comes next as a measure of “real coding work.” The post is careful not to nominate a successor — arguably because none of the candidates are obviously ready — and the 277 upvotes suggest the field knows it. Benchmarks die quietly. The thing that replaces them is usually controversial for a year before everyone agrees it was the right call.

Culture · Pure Delight
08

Magic: The Gathering took him from N2 to Japanese fluency.

A developer hits the dreaded JLPT N2 plateau — the ceiling where your textbook starts to fail you and the language stops feeling closer the harder you study. He picks up Japanese-language Magic: The Gathering, plays it badly for several months, and emerges fluent on the other side. The post is a love letter to the principle that fluency is acquired, not studied; and that the acquisition usually requires something you actually want to do in the language.

The mechanism is the most interesting part. He didn’t learn vocabulary lists. He learned card text, then strategy text, then the casual shop-talk you have with the person across the table when you’re both pretending you don’t care if you win.

Directly For You · Claude Code
09

EvanFlow: a TDD feedback loop for claude code.

A new open-source tool that wraps Claude Code in a strict test-driven feedback loop: write the failing test first, let the agent iterate, only ship the diff when the suite turns green. Small project, sharp idea — the kind of tool that gets adopted quickly because it doesn’t require you to change anything else about your workflow. You bring your tests; EvanFlow brings the discipline.

The repo is barely a week old and already has people in the comments describing it as the missing scaffolding. The shape of it suggests we are entering the era where the interesting Claude Code tooling isn’t IDE plugins or fancy dashboards — it’s the small, sharp wrappers that turn the agent into a different kind of collaborator depending on the loop you put around it.

Applies to youYou already write skills, hooks, and routines around Claude Code. EvanFlow is one extra primitive in the same direction. Star the repo, watch where it goes for a week, and decide whether to fold it into your daily loop. The whole thing is a single afternoon to try.
