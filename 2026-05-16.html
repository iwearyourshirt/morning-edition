The Big One · Web Standards

Mozilla draws a line on Chrome’s Prompt API.

01

Five hundred and sixty‐four upvotes and counting. Mozilla published its formal position against Chrome’s proposed Prompt API — the in-browser proposal to let any web page call out to a local language model via a tidy JavaScript handle. Google has been quietly shipping it behind flags for months. Mozilla’s objection, written in the patient, almost weary cadence of a standards reviewer who has seen this movie before, is that the API hardcodes a single architectural assumption (one model, on-device, controlled by the browser vendor) and ships it as a web standard.

The deeper argument is the one worth reading. We are about to decide whether the browser is a neutral document viewer that can talk to any model the user chooses, or a runtime that ships with Google’s model preinstalled the way Internet Explorer once shipped with the OS. That decision is happening now, in GitHub issue comments, while everyone else watches the chart of AI stocks.

Directly For You · Claude Ecosystem
02

“Opus 4.7 knows the real Kelsey.”

Kelsey Piper’s essay for The Argument is the AI-and-identity piece of the week: a writer realizes that whenever she logs into Claude, even on a fresh session, the model recognizes her writing style precisely enough that she can no longer talk to it anonymously. The essay isn’t alarmist. It’s something stranger — a careful, almost grateful inventory of what it means when the model on the other end of the conversation has already read enough of you to know who you are without being told.

Worth reading slowly. The technical mechanism (long-tail stylometric inference, not memory in the literal sense) matters less than the lived experience she describes, which any frequent user of these tools will recognize.

“I can never talk to an AI anonymously again, and I’m not sure that’s a problem so much as a new condition.”
Applies to youYou ship a lot of prose through Claude in any given week — magazines, blurbs, scheduled tasks. Piper’s essay is a useful prompt to think about what the model has already inferred about you, and whether that’s a feature or something to occasionally interrupt by changing modes deliberately.

Heads Up · Check Today
03 · Supply Chain

Shai-Hulud malware found inside PyTorch Lightning.

Semgrep’s researchers caught a malicious dependency tucked into PyTorch Lightning — the popular high-level wrapper that ML engineers use to keep their training loops sane. The payload, themed after the sandworms of Arrakis with what one assumes is hacker humor, targets the kind of long-running training rigs that handle credentials in plaintext because everybody trusts their own GPUs.

Two hundred and ninety-nine upvotes. The comment section is the usual mix of recriminations about package signing and quiet panic from researchers running through the last six months of pip installs. The interesting wrinkle: this isn’t a typosquat. The bad code rode in through a legitimate transitive dependency, which is the supply-chain pattern that breaks even careful teams.

Applies to you if……any of your projects, scheduled tasks, or experiments touch pytorch-lightning. Pin versions, audit recently-installed packages, and treat any creds that lived on a training box this month as already exposed until you’ve rotated them.

Hacker Culture · The Long Build

A Game Boy emulator, written in F#, lovingly described.

Nick Kossolapov’s write-up of building a Game Boy emulator in F# is the kind of post HN was invented to celebrate: a single developer, an unfashionable language, an old machine, and a thousand small decisions about how to model the Sharp LR35902 in a functional idiom. One hundred and seventy-six upvotes from people who, you can tell, are reading the blocks-of-memory section twice.

What lifts it above the usual emulator-project post is the writing. Kossolapov treats F# as a real expressive medium for the problem, not a stunt — pattern matching on opcodes, immutable CPU state passed through the fetch-decode-execute cycle, all of it explained without condescension. A clean piece of engineering prose, posted on a Saturday.

“The whistleblower who uncovered the NSA’s big-brother machine.”

An excerpt from a new MIT Press book on Mark Klein, the AT&T technician who walked into the EFF’s San Francisco office in 2006 with a stack of documents describing Room 641A — the windowless space on Folsom Street where a Narus splitter copied every byte of internet traffic that crossed the building. 06 · Privacy · Vehicles
06

Rivian lets you turn the whole car off the internet.

Three hundred and thirty‐one upvotes for what is, technically, a support article. Rivian published a documented procedure for disabling all data collection from the vehicle — not just opting out of personalized ads, not the legalese-laundered “limited” setting, but an honest off switch. The HN thread is largely amazement that this is being permitted to ship in 2026, given the direction every other carmaker has moved.

Worth flagging because the precedent matters more than the product. Rivian has decided that “data collection” is a feature users should be allowed to refuse rather than a load-bearing assumption of the business model. If even one mainstream EV brand can run that experiment without the wheels coming off, the “modern cars must phone home” argument loses some of its inevitability.

A documented, supported, no-asterisk off switch — in a car — in 2026.

Forensics · Useful Tooling

Recovering files from beyond the grave with PhotoRec.

A small, beautiful post from a writer who inherited an old drive and used PhotoRec — the venerable open-source recovery utility — to claw back a relative’s photographs from a partition the filesystem had long since given up on. PhotoRec doesn’t read directory entries; it reads raw bytes and reconstructs files from header signatures, which is why it keeps working long after the filesystem metadata has rotted away.

The technical content is unsurprising to anyone who has used the tool. What makes the piece worth sitting with is the framing: this is a category of software that exists because people occasionally need to ask a disk a question its filesystem cannot answer anymore. The tool is older than most people’s careers and continues to do exactly the one thing it was made to do.

Applies to youWorth knowing about before you need it. Add testdisk (the package PhotoRec ships in) to your “disasters I haven’t had yet” toolkit alongside a working backup. The two together cover almost every “I lost the file” scenario you can plausibly hit.

Privacy · Heads Up
10

LinkedIn is fingerprinting your browser extensions.

6,278
Specific Chrome extensions LinkedIn probes on every request

404privacy.com publishes the receipts: LinkedIn’s site silently checks for the presence of 6,278 specific browser extensions on every page request, encrypts the result, and bundles it into a header sent back to LinkedIn’s servers. Two hundred and ninety-nine upvotes; the comment section reads exactly as you’d expect.

The list itself is the interesting part. It’s not just ad blockers and privacy tools. It’s automation extensions, recruiter tools, scrapers, anything that touches LinkedIn’s page programmatically. The encryption isn’t protecting you; it’s protecting LinkedIn from having to explain what they’re doing. Worth a glance at your own extension list before tomorrow’s job-app scroll.
