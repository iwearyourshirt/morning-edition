The Big One · Claude Ecosystem

Claude Code learns to keep a routine.

01

Anthropic published documentation today for Claude Code Routines: a way to define parameterized, repeatable workflows that the agent can run autonomously, on demand or on a schedule. Five hundred and fifty-two upvotes, three hundred and nineteen comments — the kind of immediate, sustained attention that only happens when developers recognize a primitive that fits a hole they’ve been working around.

The interesting move isn’t the feature itself; it’s the timing. Skills, hooks, plugins, and now routines are arriving in the order you’d build them if you wanted the agent to gradually internalize the shape of someone’s week. Each layer makes the previous ones more valuable in retrospect.

Applies to youYou already run Cowork skills and a scheduled magazine task. Routines are the Claude Code analogue of the workflows you’ve been hand-rolling. Anything you currently chain through a terminal — deploys, audits, daily reports — is now a Routine candidate worth an afternoon of testing.

AI Tools · Pattern Recognition
02

Google ports the “Skills” idea into the browser.

Chrome announced a feature today that lets users save their best AI prompts as one-click tools available from the address bar. Right-click a page, fire your “summarize and pull action items” prompt, get a result. The branding is unsubtle: Google is calling them Skills.

The vocabulary convergence isn’t an accident. Anthropic shipped Skills, then Cowork popularized them, and now the largest browser on earth is using the same word for the same idea. What started as an Anthropic-shaped abstraction is becoming the default way the industry talks about packaged, reusable agent behavior.

“Skills” is rapidly becoming the noun that everyone agrees on for the unit of agent behavior.
Worth a lookIf you’ve already authored Cowork skills, the mental model transfers directly. Worth ten minutes to see what Google’s shape of a skill looks like — the constraints reveal what they think browser-native agents are actually for.

Heads Up · Check Today
03 · Your Data

Fiverr left customer files public and searchable.

Five hundred and twenty-three upvotes for the most disquieting Tell HN of the week: a researcher discovered that Fiverr’s customer-uploaded files — the actual deliverables and source materials passed between freelancers and clients — were sitting at predictable URLs with no authentication. Search engines had been indexing them for years.

The thread filled with people finding their own logos, draft contracts, and brand assets in the public results. Fiverr’s response, by all accounts, was slow and confused. The mechanism is the boring one: a CDN bucket configured for “public-read” from day one and never revisited. The consequence is the entire creative-services contract industry having a small but real privacy problem.

Applies to you if……you’ve ever used Fiverr, hired anyone through it, or sent files into one of its order threads. Worth a quick search of your Fiverr order history for anything proprietary, and a Google search of your name plus “fiverrcdn” while the index still has stragglers.

Foundations · Database Design

5NF is the normal form everybody skipped, and it’s quietly the right one.

An author working on a database design book published a methodical, lucid argument for fifth normal form — the one that even working DBAs tend to brush past on their way to “3NF is good enough.” The post walks through a real schema, shows where 3NF leaves loaded guns lying around, and demonstrates how 5NF eliminates an entire class of update anomalies without making anything more complicated than it already was.

It’s the rare technical post that makes you want to open the schema you’re currently shipping and look at it differently.

“Saying goodbye to Agile.”

Lewis Campbell wrote the post that a generation of working engineers has been waiting for somebody to write properly: a clear-eyed, unsentimental account of what Agile was supposed to be, what it became, and why the practice now bears almost no resemblance to the manifesto it descended from. The essay is short, specific, and refuses both the breathless devotion and the easy cynicism that usually structure this argument. The result reads like an obituary written by someone who knew the deceased well enough to be honest about them.

AI Research · Architecture Question

Introspective Diffusion Language Models.

A research group has published work on language models built on diffusion rather than autoregressive next-token prediction — with the additional twist that the model can inspect and revise its own intermediate reasoning states. Two hundred and fifty-eight upvotes; the paper itself is dense, the claims are measured, and the comment section is unusually serious.

The interesting subtext is that the question of whether autoregressive is right is back on the table. For two years it has felt settled. The fact that careful researchers are publishing alternatives that don’t look like marketing is a small but real signal that the architectural conversation is opening back up. None of this displaces what you’re using today; all of it changes what the field looks like in eighteen months.

09

Meet plain — full-stack Python for humans and agents.

Dropseed shipped Plain, a full-stack Python framework that explicitly designs its API surface for two consumers: developers and AI agents. That second audience is the interesting half. The framework treats agent-readability — predictable conventions, machine-friendly errors, introspectable structure — as a first-class concern rather than something bolted on after the fact.

Whether Plain is the right framework is a separate question; what it represents is hard to ignore. The next generation of web frameworks won’t just be designed for engineers. They’ll be designed for the agents engineers deploy on top of them.

Closing Reflection · Hacker Archaeology

Fourteen years of not knowing what you were testing.

14
Years a test suite lied silently before anyone caught it

An archaeology dive on llama.gs reconstructs the history of a piece of educational software, an Apple II clone built in the East Germany of 1989, and the long quiet bug that turned its accompanying test harness into a passive co-conspirator. For fourteen years, every run reported success without actually running the assertions it claimed to run. Nobody noticed because the failure mode was indistinguishable from the success mode, which is a sentence that deserves to be carved over the door of every QA department in the world.

The post is two things at once: a wry piece of computing history and a small, sharp warning. Tests are only worth what their failures cost. A test that cannot fail is just a comment with overhead.
