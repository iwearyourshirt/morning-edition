The Big One · Hacker Culture

Ghostty is leaving GitHub.

01

Mitchell Hashimoto announced this morning that Ghostty — his terminal emulator and one of the most-watched indie projects on the platform — is migrating off GitHub. The post is calm, methodical, and damning in the unflashy way only Hashimoto can pull off. Two thousand one hundred upvotes and six hundred and fifty comments later, half of HN is asking the same question out loud: if Mitchell is leaving, what exactly is GitHub still for?

The piece itself is less manifesto than ledger. He details the migration choices, what stays, what goes, what he’s willing to lose. The subtext is the entire point. The single most important repository host on Earth has accumulated enough small frictions, AI-generated noise, and quiet erosions of trust that one of its highest-profile users has done the math and walked.

Directly For You · Claude Ecosystem

Anthropic introduces Claude for Creative Work.

Anthropic dropped a positioning piece today framing Claude as a tool not just for code and analysis, but for the messier creative-professional stack — writing, design, video, ideation. The announcement is the company stepping into territory it had been quietly serving anyway, and acknowledging it out loud.

The interesting part isn’t the marketing. It’s the implicit road map. When Anthropic explicitly names creative work as a category they’re building for, you can read the next eighteen months of features in reverse: better long-form drafting, richer multimodal handling, more deliberate support for the kinds of recursive workflows creative people actually run.

“The category is no longer just adjacent — it’s the road map.”
Applies to youThis is your stack. The Cowork-plus-skills-plus-scheduled-task pattern you’ve been running is exactly what this announcement is signaling support for. Worth reading slowly to figure out which of your existing workarounds are about to become first-class features.

Heads Up · Check Today
03 · Claude Code

A regression is making Claude Code subagents refuse work.

An issue filed against the claude-code repo today documents a regression: the malware-policy reminder injected on every file read is causing subagents to refuse legitimate tasks — flagging ordinary user code as suspicious and bailing mid-job. The thread is one hundred and eighty-six points and seventy-nine comments deep, with the kind of careful, reproducible bug reports that suggest this is not a one-off.

The behavior is probabilistic, which is the nasty part. You will not necessarily hit it. When you do, the failure mode is silent-ish refusal, not a hard error. A long agent run completes “successfully” with key steps quietly skipped. That’s the worst kind of regression because you cannot tell, after the fact, which of your last few sessions were actually clean.

Applies to youYou run subagent-heavy workflows daily. Until this is patched: keep an eye on long agent runs for signs of mid-task refusal. If a job finishes much faster than expected with thin results, that’s the symptom. The issue thread itself is the best place to track the fix.

Useful · The AirDrop Question

LocalSend: AirDrop, but it works between everything.

Seven hundred and eighty-one upvotes for a quiet open-source project that does one thing exceedingly well: ship files between phones, laptops, and tablets across iOS, Android, macOS, Windows, and Linux without a cloud round-trip and without an account. No relay servers, no compression, no sign-in — just LAN discovery and a transfer.

The reason this hit the front page in 2026 is the reason it always hits the front page: AirDrop is still the cleanest UX in computing, and Apple has spent twenty years declining to let it cross the pond. LocalSend is what the obvious solution looks like when nobody owns the protocol.

Applies to youIf you regularly pass files between your phone, your laptop, and machines that are not all Apple, this replaces a lot of small workflow friction. Install it on every device once, forget it’s there, use it forever.

06 · Privacy · Worth Reading
06

A coalition is forming to keep your phone yours.

Eleven hundred and eighty-six upvotes — the highest-engagement story on the front page after Ghostty — for a coalition site arguing that upcoming European mobile-platform rules will, in their current draft, quietly end the practice of installing software on your own Android phone without going through a vendor. The campaign is from developers, not lobbyists, and reads like one. Plain language, screenshots, before-and-after diagrams. No call to outrage, just a request to understand the mechanism.

The substance underneath is the substance Hacker News has been circling around for years: who, exactly, owns a device once you’ve bought it. The answer in 2026 keeps shrinking. This is the latest, tidiest articulation of why that matters even if you don’t sideload anything yourself.

A device you cannot install software on is, increasingly, a device you do not own.

Neuroscience · New Mechanism

A single experience can rewire the brain.

Quanta is reporting on a wave of neuroscience papers describing a previously underappreciated form of plasticity — behavioral timescale synaptic plasticity — that lets the hippocampus reorganize its place-cell circuitry on the basis of a single salient experience. Not repeated practice, not consolidation across nights of sleep. One event. The synapses that fire during the experience and the ones that fired seconds before or after get linked, and a memory is built in essentially one shot.

What makes this more than a fun fact is the timescale. The mechanism operates over seconds, not milliseconds, which is the awkward in-between window where most existing synaptic-plasticity stories had nothing useful to say. If the result holds, a real chunk of how learning is taught in textbooks will need to be redrawn. The article is patient and careful and worth the slow read.

Strange Object · Pure Delight
08

A 13B-parameter language model that only knows 1930.

Six hundred and sixty points and two hundred and seventy-two comments for Talkie, a language model trained exclusively on text that existed before January 1st, 1930 — newspapers, novels, letters, scientific journals, the lot. Ask it about cars and it talks about the new Ford. Ask it about war and it talks about the last one. Ask it about quantum mechanics and you can watch it reach for vocabulary it does not have.

It is, on the merits, a serious experiment in cultural capture and a quietly devastating reminder that LLMs are exactly the texts you train them on, no more, no less. It is also, on a less serious axis, the first chatbot in years that someone actually wants to talk to.

Dev Tools · Terminal
09

Warp open-sources its terminal.

Warp announced today that it’s open-sourcing its terminal — the AI-native one with the block-based UI and the agent panel that a non-trivial slice of HN has been quietly using for two years now. Two hundred and sixteen points, sixty-four comments, most of them some flavor of about time.

The interesting subplot here is timing. Warp opens up the same week Ghostty leaves GitHub and Anthropic publishes “Claude for Creative Work.” Read the three together and you can almost see the edges of the new terminal stack: AI-native, locally driven, less beholden to any one vendor’s platform. The terminal, for years the most boring corner of the developer toolkit, is suddenly the place where the most interesting fights are happening.

Applies to youWorth a look if you’re still on the default terminal. Warp’s agent integration is the closest non-Claude-Code thing to the workflow you already use; an open-source baseline makes it a much safer bet to adopt.

A single CVE can pop the largest code host on Earth.

10.0
CVSS score · CVE-2026-3854 · GitHub RCE

Wiz published a deep technical breakdown of CVE-2026-3854, a remote-code-execution vulnerability in GitHub’s own infrastructure that hit a perfect ten on the severity scale. Three hundred points and seventy-three comments deep, and the comments are unusually quiet — the kind of quiet that means people are reading instead of arguing.

GitHub has patched it. The post is therefore a postmortem, not a warning. But the shape of the bug — a chain of small, individually reasonable design choices that combined into a worst-case outcome — is the kind of thing worth absorbing slowly. Every system you build has the same possible chain in it. The only question is whether anybody has bothered to enumerate it yet.
