The Big One · Privacy · Applies to You

Chrome quietly installed a 4 GB AI model on your machine.

01

Thirteen hundred and fifty-nine upvotes. Nine hundred and eleven comments. The biggest story on Hacker News by a wide margin, and one of those rare cases where the headline does not oversell. A researcher at thatprivacyguy.com noticed Chrome had silently downloaded a four-gigabyte on-device model — Gemini Nano — without an opt-in, a permission prompt, or even a changelog mention. Most people would have no idea it was there.

The defense will be that the model is for “built-in AI features” and runs locally, and the defense is partly true. The complaint is that four gigabytes is not a rounding error on a laptop SSD, that consent is a feature and not a courtesy, and that the precedent — ship a multi-billion-parameter model into hundreds of millions of devices without asking — is going to age the way silent telemetry aged.

Applies to youYou use Chrome. The model is sitting at ~/Library/Application Support/Google/Chrome/OptimizationGuide. If you don’t want it, you can delete it — though the post documents that Chrome may simply re-download it. Worth a once-over before you plug in the laptop tonight.

Heads Up · Internet Weather
03 · DNS Outage

.de went dark for hours after a DNSSEC misstep.

Five hundred and ninety-one upvotes. Two hundred and ninety-six comments. Late yesterday, the .de top-level domain — one of the largest country-code TLDs on Earth, sitting underneath millions of German websites — effectively went offline for resolvers that strictly validate DNSSEC. The cause appears to be a signing or key-rollover error inside DENIC’s DNSSEC chain.

DNSSEC is the cryptographic layer that proves a DNS answer came from the rightful authority and was not tampered with. It is also, depending on whom you ask, either the load-bearing wall of internet trust or a fragile machine that periodically takes entire countries off the air. Yesterday’s incident is an argument for both readings at once.

Agents · New Capability

Cloudflare lets agents sign up, buy domains, and deploy.

Cloudflare announced today that AI agents can now create Cloudflare accounts, attach Stripe billing, purchase domains, and ship Workers projects autonomously — the entire lifecycle from cold start to live URL, with no human in the seat. The HN thread spent a hundred and sixty-one points and seventy-five comments arguing about exactly which kind of milestone this is. A useful one. A dangerous one. Both.

What is unambiguous is the direction of travel. The infrastructure to let an agent stand up its own publicly addressable artifact — not just write code, but ship it — now exists at a name-brand provider. Whatever your priors on agent autonomy, that floor just moved.

Applies to youYou already wire agents into your own toolchain. The Cloudflare flow is the cleanest published end-to-end example yet of an agent deploying a real product, billing and all. Worth twenty minutes reading the post even if you don’t adopt it.

06 · Agents · Cost Reality Check
06

Computer use is forty-five times more expensive than a structured API.

The Reflex team published a careful empirical post measuring what every agent builder suspects in their gut: when an agent has to drive a screen instead of calling a clean API, the unit economics collapse. Their measured ratio is forty-five to one. Three hundred and sixty-nine points on HN, two hundred and fourteen comments, very little disagreement on the result and a lot of disagreement on what to do about it.

The honest read is that screen-driving is a bridge technology. It is what you reach for when the API does not exist or refuses to grant your agent access. It is not the destination. The destination is APIs everywhere, with auth flows that treat agents as first-class citizens. Until then, you pay the forty-five-times tax for the privilege of clicking buttons that should have been endpoints.

Screen-driving is a bridge technology. The destination is APIs everywhere.

AI Research · Inference Speed

Multi-token prediction comes to Gemma 4.

Google’s research blog detailed a new technique for accelerating Gemma 4 inference: multi-token prediction drafters that let the model speculatively emit several tokens at once and verify them in parallel. Five hundred and twenty-two points on HN, two hundred and thirty-four comments, with the usual mix of careful praise and skepticism about how much of the speedup survives outside benchmark conditions.

The bigger story is that the inference-speed frontier has decisively moved from “quantize harder” to “rethink the autoregressive loop.” Speculative decoding, drafter models, parallel sampling — these are no longer exotic optimizations from research labs. They are the standard playbook now. The language models you use in eighteen months will feel different not because they got bigger, but because they stopped waiting on themselves.

Storage Frontier
10

A single SSD now holds nearly a quarter-petabyte.

245TB
Capacity of a single Micron 6600 ION data-center drive

Micron began shipping the 6600 ION today — a single 2.5-inch data-center SSD with two hundred and forty-five terabytes of usable capacity. Thirty-one points on HN, a small thread by the standards of this front page, but the number itself is the story. A drive that fits in your palm now holds more bytes than the entire Library of Congress digitized text collection, with several spare orders of magnitude.

Storage gets cheaper and denser every year, and every year the headline number sounds preposterous until it stops sounding preposterous, usually about a week after it ships. We are firmly past the point where any single human can fill one of these in a lifetime. The market is no longer pricing them for humans.
