The Argument · Sharp Essay
02

“AI is slowing down.”

Ed Zitron’s latest at Where’s Your Ed At made the front page with five hundred and four upvotes and a comment section twice that size. The thesis is unfashionable and carefully argued: the rate of meaningful capability improvement in frontier models has flattened, the released benchmarks are increasingly contested, and the economics of the buildout no longer pencil unless you assume a category of progress that nobody is currently demonstrating.

It’s the kind of essay you read twice. Once to disagree with reflexively. Then once more to figure out which specific claims you actually disagree with, and which ones you were just pattern-matching against.

“The benchmarks are the part of this you should trust the least, and the part of this the industry leans on the most.”
Read with a clear headYou’ve been building daily workflows on top of frontier models. Whether or not Zitron is right, his methodology for separating real capability gains from product polish is the most useful frame on this question published this quarter.

Heads Up · Privacy
03 · Surveillance

Signal to the UK: surveillance is not safety.

Five hundred and twenty-six points for a PDF Signal published in response to the United Kingdom’s latest attempt to compel client-side scanning in encrypted messengers. The argument is measured and the position is not. Signal will not weaken end-to-end encryption. If the law passes as drafted, Signal will leave the country.

The document is unusually clear-eyed for this kind of policy fight. It separates what the proposed legislation says it does (catches bad actors) from what it actually does (gives every future government a permanent on-device surveillance hook), and it does so in language a policymaker can’t reasonably claim was inaccessible.

Applies to youYou use Signal. If you have friends or family in the UK on Signal, this affects your shared threat model right now, not someday. The PDF is short. Read it before the news cycle does the summarizing for you.

Dev Tools · Apple Goes Open

Apple ships a public Core AI Framework for third-party models.

Quietly tucked into yesterday’s Apple Intelligence rollout: a new Core AI developer framework that lets any app run, swap, and orchestrate on-device models, including non-Apple ones. Two hundred and sixty-seven points and a sober comment thread of developers reading the docs in real time and concluding, with caution, that this is the move people have been waiting for since the original Core ML.

The interesting bit isn’t the framework itself. It’s that Apple chose to publish a primitive flexible enough to host models it didn’t train and can’t control. That’s a posture change.

Applies to youAnything you build that runs locally on a Mac — transcription, summarization, image work — this is the framework you’ll be targeting in twelve months. Worth scanning the docs now while the surface area is small.

Weird Science · First Principles

“Why are cells small?”

An essay from burrito.bio walks, with patient first-principles reasoning, through the question of why life chose to be assembled from tiny things rather than a few large ones. The answer involves surface-area-to-volume ratios, diffusion limits, and the brute thermodynamics of moving molecules around. None of it is new. All of it is beautifully explained. The pleasure here is not novelty. It’s clarity, applied to a question you stopped asking in seventh grade.

AI Research · Coding Agents

Cognition introduces FrontierCode.

The team behind Devin published a research note this morning describing FrontierCode — a framework for evaluating coding agents against the kinds of tasks production engineers actually spend their days on, rather than the leetcode-shaped problems most benchmarks rely on. One hundred and fifty-eight points and a comment thread that is, for once, more skeptical than the original post.

The contribution worth taking seriously is methodological. FrontierCode insists that an agent should be measured on tasks with ambiguity in the spec, dependencies on services that change behavior between runs, and the absence of a single correct diff. That’s a more honest definition of the problem than “solve this exercise.” Whether Cognition’s own scores hold up is a separate question. The framing is the contribution.

Dev Tools · Show HN
09

Gitdot: git push to something that isn’t GitHub.

Two hundred and twenty points for Gitdot, a fully open-source GitHub alternative written in Rust. The pitch is straightforward: GitHub’s feature set is now table stakes, the lock-in is meaningful, and the underlying protocol is decades old and open. The reason a new entrant looks plausible is that the surface area to clone is no longer the hard part. The hard part is community.

The implementation is fast, the UI is restrained, and the comment thread is the predictable mix of “I will switch tomorrow” and “you will not switch tomorrow.” Both groups are probably right. Worth a bookmark.

The Number · Throughput
10

Xiaomi’s MiMo claims a trillion parameters at a thousand tokens per second.

1,000
Tokens per second, on a model with one trillion parameters

Five hundred and forty-six points for a Xiaomi blog post announcing MiMo-v2.5-Pro-UltraSpeed — a one-trillion-parameter model serving at one thousand tokens per second. The number is the story. If the benchmark holds up under independent measurement, the throughput is several multiples beyond what comparable Western frontier models are publishing, on hardware that’s not yet been disclosed.

Treat the claim as provisional. Treat the trajectory as not. The interesting question isn’t whether the number is correct today. It’s how long the gap between announcement and reproduction is going to keep shrinking.
