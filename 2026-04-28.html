The Big One · AI & History

Someone built a 13B language model that thinks it’s 1930.

01

A research team has trained a thirteen-billion-parameter language model and tuned it to sound like 1930. Not a parlour trick, not a costume on top of GPT — a fully working LLM, hosted online, fluent in the cadence, vocabulary, and certainties of pre-war English. Three hundred and twenty-five upvotes on Hacker News by morning, and a comment thread split between delight, technical scrutiny, and quiet awe.

The premise is more useful than it looks. By constraining a model to one historical decade, Talkie isolates style from content in a way frontier LLMs almost can’t. The result is a kind of acoustic fossil: a snapshot of how a language sounded before television, before the Cold War, before the entire idea of being chronically online. The interesting question isn’t whether Talkie is correct about 1930. It’s how much of meaning turns out to be encoded in the era a sentence was written in.

AI Ecosystem · Pricing Shift
02

GitHub Copilot moves to usage-based billing.

GitHub announced this week that Copilot is moving off its flat monthly subscription and onto metered, usage-based pricing — a free token allowance, then per-request charges from there. Six hundred and thirty-seven points, four hundred and seventy-five comments. The thread is, predictably, a referendum: half of it furious about variable bills, half of it resigned to the fact that this is where every serious AI tool eventually ends up.

Every shovel seller hits the same fork in the road. Priced like SaaS, or priced like infrastructure. Once a tool gets expensive enough to run, the choice makes itself. Copilot, having reached that point, is now telling its users out loud what most AI products will tell theirs within the year.

“Once the inference bill is real money, the subscription model stops being an option. It becomes a subsidy.”
Worth watchingYou’re a Claude Code and Cowork user, not a Copilot one. But this is the first big-name AI dev tool to formally re-price as infrastructure, and it’s a preview of the conversation everyone in the space will be having by Q3. Read the comments for what developers actually tolerate before they jump.

Heads Up · Before You Upgrade
03 · macOS 27

macOS 27 is rewriting how your Mac talks to the network.

Eclectic Light Co. has compiled the networking changes coming in macOS 27, and there are more than the usual incremental tightening. Several legacy interfaces are being retired. The firewall picks up per-app routing controls. Some VPN configurations will need to be rewritten, and a category of apps that talk to local-network resources will be prompted for explicit consent on first run.

Two hundred and twenty-six points; one hundred and ninety-nine comments, plenty of horror stories from people whose corporate VPNs already broke during the developer seed. This is one of those releases where reading the post before installing is genuinely cheaper than not.

Applies to youYou live on a Mac. Read this before macOS 27 hits the public release in the fall — especially if you run a VPN, custom DNS, or any tool that reaches local-network services. A few of the changes will require config adjustments, and a few apps will simply stop working until they’re updated.

Open Source · End Of An Era

pgBackRest, the spine of Postgres backups, is no longer being maintained.

pgBackRest — the de-facto open-source backup tool for Postgres, the one quietly running underneath a meaningful slice of the world’s production databases — has been put into maintenance mode by its primary author. Four hundred and seventeen upvotes, two hundred and twenty comments, most of them eulogies from engineers whose stacks have leaned on this tool for the better part of a decade.

Open source has its own rhythm. A tool quietly becomes essential. The maintainer quietly burns out. The community panics. And then either someone steps up or the alternatives get serious. We’re at step three. The next few weeks of GitHub forks will tell you which way step four goes.

06 · Toy Essay · Color Perception
06

Where exactly does your blue end?

A simple question, hosted at ismy.blue: a slider, a color, and a button asking you to commit — green or blue? The site collects responses by the millions and shows you, with a single distribution curve, where your boundary falls relative to everyone else’s. Four hundred and ninety-nine points, three hundred and forty-four comments, and one of the cleaner toy-essays of the year.

What it actually demonstrates is the moment your private sense of a word and someone else’s public sense of it stop overlapping. It happens, on average, somewhere around 480 nanometers. Yours probably falls a little surprising, in one direction or the other. Take the test on the way to a meeting.

The boundary between your blue and your green is the most quietly opinionated thing about you.

Sharp Essay · Engineering Subfields

On the quiet resurgence of RF engineering.

After two decades of being the unfashionable subfield, radio-frequency engineering is having a moment. The piece on bearblog runs through what’s driving it: 5G and 6G infrastructure, satellite IoT, software-defined radio at consumer prices, and a generation of EE graduates who quite suddenly find themselves in demand. One hundred and eighty-eight upvotes; the comments fill with people listing their favorite SDR tutorials and the bench setups they’ve been quietly building in spare bedrooms.

The author’s larger point is more interesting than the trend itself. Every era has a quiet engineering subfield doing real work that nobody outside it sees. RF was one of those for fifteen years. Now the field is loud enough to hear, which means the next fifteen years of consumer products are going to be shaped by people who’ve been thinking about antennas the whole time.

Wonderful Science · Medicine
08

Sound, restored — by gene therapy.

The FDA has approved the first gene therapy for genetic hearing loss. A single dose, delivered via an engineered viral vector, ferries a working copy of the OTOF gene into the inner ear. Two hundred and thirty-nine points; the comment section is full of hearing-impaired developers asking, with carefully managed hope, whether the therapy applies to their specific mutation.

The headline is the milestone. The quieter story is the National Priority Voucher program that pulled this approval forward — a rare instance of a regulatory mechanism designed to accelerate worthy treatments doing exactly that.

Security · Reference Material
09

GTFOBins: every Unix binary is a /bin/sh in disguise.

GTFOBins is a curated index of common Unix binaries that can, given the right invocation, be coaxed into executing arbitrary shell commands — bypassing local restrictions on misconfigured systems. vi, find, tar, awk, git: the list of supposedly innocent tools that turn into escape hatches is much longer than most defenders assume.

Ninety-eight points and a small but vocal comment section reminded everyone that this resource has been quietly indispensable for both attackers and defenders for years. The list is a good reality check. Half the binaries on your system are, given the right argument, doors. Hardening a box means knowing which ones.

The Number That Stops You
10

A breach at Mercor exposes the voices of forty thousand AI contractors.

4 TB
Voice recordings exfiltrated from a single AI training-data marketplace

Mercor — an AI training-data marketplace that pays contractors to record their voices for model fine-tuning — disclosed a breach this week that exposed roughly four terabytes of audio belonging to forty thousand people. Five hundred and fifteen points; the comment section split between voice-cloning panic and the larger question of what consent actually means inside the AI training pipeline.

The numbers are the part to sit with. Forty thousand voices is a lot of training data — for legitimate models, but also for whatever a motivated attacker might want to clone. The next decade of voice-deepfake fraud has a slightly clearer source attribution this morning.

Applies to you if……you ever recorded audio for an AI training-data marketplace, your samples may be in this set. Mercor will have notification details on their breach page. If you use any voice-authentication system — a few banks, a few authorization tools — freezing or disabling that factor today is a low-cost protection against the inevitable downstream use.
