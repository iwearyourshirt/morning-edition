Heads Up · The Panopticon
03 · Surveillance

Meta will start logging its own employees’ keystrokes to train its AI.

Reuters reported, and Hacker News promptly inhaled, that Meta will begin capturing its employees’ mouse movements and keystrokes to generate training data for its future models. Six hundred and twenty‐seven points. Four hundred and twenty‐nine comments. Most of them a variation of no thank you.

Bracket the outrage for a second. The interesting piece is the precedent. This is the first-party workplace‐surveillance feedback loop made explicit: the people building the model become, by policy, the training corpus for it. The question of whether the keyboard is an acceptable data source used to be philosophical. It’s operational now.

Signal, not actionYou don’t work at Meta — this is a weather report, not a to-do. But the policy will be copied. Watch for the version of this that shows up inside tools you already use, packaged as “product improvement” or “analytics.”

Security · Post‐Mortem

The Vercel breach, in detail.

Trend Micro published a full technical write‐up of the Vercel breach this week: an OAuth supply‐chain attack that reached into the platform’s environment variables — the storage layer where secrets, API keys, and database credentials live for every application deployed on top of it. Three hundred and twenty‐three points on Hacker News, one hundred and nine comments, most of them working through the exact shape of the attack chain.

The mechanism is the lesson. OAuth integrations with third‐party services create a class of compromise that bypasses the platform’s own authentication model entirely. It is not that Vercel was careless. It is that the entire pattern of connect your GitHub, connect your Sentry, connect your database bakes this surface into the system, and every deployment platform that uses the same pattern has the same exposure.

Applies to you if……you deploy anything to Vercel. Rotate API keys this week and audit your connected integrations — the breach chain ran through OAuth grants, so the review starts with who has access to what and works outward from there.

cal.diy: self-host the scheduler.

Cal.com released cal.diy — a community‐maintained, self‐hostable edition of their scheduling product, open‐sourced under a permissive license. Two hundred and twenty‐three points, fifty‐four comments, most of them already planning where in their homelab the container will live by the weekend.
