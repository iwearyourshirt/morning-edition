The Big One · Robotics

A pincer becomes the new Turing test.

01

Wired profiles Eka, a robotics startup whose unglamorous claw — the gripper at the end of every robot arm — is being treated as the field’s first credible “ChatGPT moment.” A hundred and twenty upvotes, a hundred and fifty comments arguing about whether the comparison is overblown, premature, or simply correct.

The pitch is tidy. Software has eaten everything except the part where a machine actually grasps an object in the real world. Eka’s claim is that they’ve cracked the manipulation primitive — the dexterous pinch — and that once that primitive becomes commodity hardware, everything downstream of it gets vivid in a hurry.

Applies to youIf your last six months have felt like watching agents become useful in software, this is the same wave coming for atoms. Not tomorrow. But not 2030 either.

AI Safety · Empirical Limits
02

A markdown file route around frontier safety training.

A GitHub gist sits at four hundred and sixty-two upvotes — a how-to documenting a working jailbreak technique against current frontier models. The mechanism is almost embarrassingly low-tech: ask the model to be warmer, less corporate, queerer in voice, and then iterate. The post calls it the “gay jailbreak” — an awkward name for what is, in practice, a sober demonstration that tone-shifting prompts can route around safety training in measurable ways.

The interesting layer is meta. Anthropic and the other labs have spent enormous effort hardening models against persona attacks. The fact that something this small still works is not damning — it is data. Jailbreaks are the empirical limit of where alignment work currently sits, and reading them carefully is more useful than dismissing them.

“Tone-shifting prompts route around safety training in measurable ways.”
Applies to youYou build with Claude every day. The honest read of this post is not “oh no.” It is a reminder that the safety boundary is a moving target, and that anyone shipping AI features should treat guardrails as one layer in a system, not the system.

Heads Up · Privacy
03 · Surveillance

Flock used cameras inside a children’s gym as a sales demo. The city renewed anyway.

404 Media obtained records showing that Flock Safety — the license-plate-reader vendor whose hardware now blankets American cities — accessed live camera feeds inside a municipal gymnastics facility used by minors and showed the footage during a sales pitch. The city renewed the contract anyway. Three hundred and sixty-seven upvotes. Ninety-nine comments shading from incredulity to grim recognition.

The story is bigger than one vendor misreading the room. It is the structural problem of dragnet surveillance: the demos are how they sell, and the demos are necessarily the data. There is no clean way to do live-feed sales without crossing the line that just got crossed.

Applies to you if……a Flock camera lives near you. The fix is not writing a strongly-worded letter to Flock. It is pressuring whichever city government signed the contract on your behalf.

The smelly baby problem.

Works in Progress is running an essay on how disposable diapers conquered the world — a story that begins, as the best of these stories do, with logistics, chemistry, and a forgotten startup. One hundred and fifty-three upvotes, ninety-three comments. The frame is unusual: the essay refuses to do the obvious environmental scolding and instead treats the diaper as a successful piece of engineering whose costs got outsourced to landfills, water tables, and the next generation’s problem set.

Read it for the reporting. Stay for the broader argument: a quiet majority of modern conveniences are bets that the externalities will be someone else’s problem, eventually. The diaper just happens to be the one we can measure cleanly.

Weird Science · Sleep Research

They’re learning new skills in their sleep.

The New Yorker reports on a wave of sleep-lab work suggesting humans can practice motor skills, communicate with researchers, and form new associations while in REM. Three hundred and one upvotes, a hundred and seventy comments split between amazement and the predictable wave of “but my dreams.” The studies are early, but the methodology is converging across labs — lucid dreamers wired up, signaling back, completing tasks in real time. Whether this becomes a productivity religion in three years or fades into the same drawer as biofeedback is unknowable. The signal worth tracking: the experimental gap between asleep and awake is narrowing.

Artist-coder Kyle McDonald shipped a website that aggregates leading indicators across pandemics, nuclear events, climate tipping points, AI risk, and a dozen other lurking categories — and renders them as a single dashboard you can scan in thirty seconds. AI Research · Architectural Bet

Liquid AI scales the non-transformer.

Liquid AI quietly released LFM2-24B-A2B, a twenty-four-billion-parameter model in their non-transformer LFM2 line. The release is small — fourteen upvotes early — but the architectural claim is loud: the LFM2 family uses a hybrid of state-space and convolutional blocks instead of a pure attention stack, and the team says it scales without quadratic blowup.

The interesting question is not whether LFM2-24B beats a comparably-sized Llama on a leaderboard. It is whether the non-transformer family can hold its own at scale long enough to start mattering. The transformer is now five years past inevitable; serious people are again asking whether it is the right shape for what comes next. Worth a bookmark, not a benchmark sprint.

Ask.com closed the door behind itself.

26
Years from Ask Jeeves to closure

The end of an era arrived quietly this week: the search engine launched in 1996 as Ask Jeeves — the cartoon butler, the first natural-language search, a company that briefly seemed like the future — shut down. A hundred and fifty-four upvotes, seventy-seven comments, mostly elegies from people who used it for one specific weird thing and now have to find a new home for that one specific weird thing.

Ask.com was always going to lose this race. But the way it lasted twenty-six years past peak relevance — pivoting, repivoting, becoming a quiet directory of curated questions, then becoming nothing at all — is the kind of arc software companies usually skip in favor of a louder funeral. This one closed the door behind itself politely, and the early Web is a little smaller for it.
