The Big One · Anthropic Research

Anthropic teaches Claude to narrate its own thoughts.

01

Anthropic published research this week on Natural Language Autoencoders — a method for taking the dense, high-dimensional state Claude carries around mid-thought and translating it back into readable English. Two hundred and forty-nine upvotes on Hacker News, eighty comments, and a thread of researchers immediately working out the implications.

The premise is elegant. Train a small companion model to compress a chunk of Claude’s internal activations into a sentence, then decompress that sentence and check whether the result still steers Claude toward the same answer. If it does, you have a written record of what the model was thinking, in a form a human can read. The phrase natural language is doing real work here: it’s not a probe, it’s a translation.

Applies to youYou build with Claude every day. This is the long arc of interpretability research starting to bend toward something usable — the foundation for tools that will eventually let you ask why the agent did what it did and get an honest answer.

Agent Design · The Quiet Reframe

Agents need control flow, not more prompts.

The thesis of bsuh’s post is the kind of unfashionable claim that ages well: most of the problems people are currently trying to solve with longer system prompts and cleverer context engineering are, in fact, problems with their control flow. Loops, branches, retries, and explicit state — the boring scaffolding around the model — do more for reliability than another paragraph of instructions.

Four hundred and fifteen points and two hundred comments later, the thread is mostly people who have already learned this the hard way nodding emphatically. It’s a short read. It will save someone, somewhere, a week.

Applies to youYou orchestrate agents through Cowork skills and scheduled tasks — this is the architecture you’re already building toward. Worth reading just for the vocabulary.

06 · AI Research · DeepMind
06

AlphaEvolve quietly improved a dozen open problems.

DeepMind put out a long blog post detailing where AlphaEvolve — their Gemini-driven coding agent that proposes, runs, and refines algorithms in a loop — has actually moved the needle. The list is unshowy: incremental gains on data-center scheduling, faster matrix multiplication routines, a handful of small but real open-problem improvements in pure math. Two hundred and seventy-four points and a hundred and eighteen comments, mostly engineers parsing the methodology rather than the press release.

What’s worth noting is the framing. DeepMind isn’t pitching this as artificial general anything. They’re pitching it as a search procedure with very good intuitions, deployed against problems where verifying an answer is cheap and finding one is hard. That’s the right framing. It also describes most of the productive uses of agents you’ll see this year.

Weird Science · Neuroscience

The anaesthetized brain, it turns out, is still listening.

Researchers at Baylor College of Medicine recorded directly from the hippocampus of patients under general anaesthesia and found something that complicates the standard story of what unconsciousness is. The hippocampus, while the rest of the brain is supposed to be off, was tracking spoken language — not just registering sound but doing the kind of higher-order processing associated with comprehension. And the patterns weren’t static; they showed plasticity, the brain’s machinery for learning, still humming away beneath the medication.

The implications are careful and partial. It is not the case that anaesthetized patients are awake in any meaningful sense. It is the case that one of the brain’s memory structures is doing more work, under deeper sedation, than anyone had reason to expect. Eighty-eight points; thirty-four comments; a thread of neurologists arguing politely about what this means for memory consolidation theories. The kind of paper that matters more in five years than it does today.
