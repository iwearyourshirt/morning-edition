The Big One · Identity & AI

Opus 4.7 knows the real Kelsey.

01

A writer at The Argument set out to test a quiet premise: that the new generation of frontier models retains enough about us, across enough surfaces, that anonymity in front of them is becoming a fiction. She logged out, opened a fresh session, refused to identify herself — and Claude Opus 4.7 still figured out who she was, by inference, in remarkably few turns. The piece is part essay, part reckoning, and the comment section reads like a community noticing the same thing at once.

This isn’t a story about a leak. It’s a story about what models can reconstruct from the ambient shape of your prose — vocabulary, references, the way you punctuate. Anonymity used to mean not putting your name on something. Increasingly it means not having a style.

Applies to youYou spend hours a day talking to Claude across projects. The inference surface is wider than you think. Worth knowing before you reach for a “fresh chat” the next time you want to ask something off-the-record.

Long Read · Surveillance
02

The man who told us about Room 641A.

An excerpt from a forthcoming MIT Press book recounts how Mark Klein, an AT&T technician working in a windowless splitter room in downtown San Francisco, walked into the EFF’s office in 2006 carrying a manila folder. Inside was the schematic for a fiber-optic beam splitter, installed in a locked room at 611 Folsom, that copied the entire stream of internet traffic passing through and handed it to the NSA. He had no political agenda. He had blueprints.

What the piece captures, almost twenty years on, is how strange the moment looks now. A single technician, walking up the stairs of a nonprofit, was the entire mechanism by which the country found out. The infrastructure he described didn’t go away. The conditions under which someone like him could come forward arguably did.

“He had no political agenda. He had blueprints.”

03 · Supply Chain

A Shai-Hulud worm slithered into PyTorch Lightning.

Semgrep’s research team disclosed a malicious dependency planted inside PyTorch Lightning, one of the most widely used AI training libraries in Python. The payload is themed after the Shai-Hulud campaign that has been sweeping through npm and PyPI for the past several months — an automated, self-propagating worm that scrapes credentials, plants further packages, and quietly moves laterally across registries.

This one is particularly ugly because of where it landed: anyone fine-tuning a model, running training scripts in a notebook, or pulling a fresh requirements file on a research box could have ingested it. Three hundred upvotes and ninety-seven comments deep, the consensus is that the era of casually pip install-ing AI libraries in a clean venv is over.

Applies to you if……you have a Python environment that has touched pytorch-lightning recently — even transitively, even on a sandbox you forgot about. Pin your versions, rotate any tokens that lived on that machine, and read the Semgrep post before your Sunday coffee gets cold.

Creative Tools · Mechanical Joy

Jeff Bridges made a panoramic film camera.

The actor — long known to anyone who follows photo culture as a serious black-and-white panoramic shooter — spent the past few years quietly working with a small team to build the Wideluxx: a mechanical, fully analog swing-lens panoramic camera in the lineage of the old Widelux F8 that Bridges has carried around movie sets for decades. No batteries. No firmware. A spring-driven lens that sweeps across a 35mm frame and gives you a 24×58mm panorama with the soft falloff only a real swing-lens can produce.

It is, as launches go, exactly the sort of thing the internet should be making more of: small team, single object, made because the maker wanted one to exist.

Applies to youYou shoot a lot. This is a camera designed by someone who shoots a lot. Even if you never buy one, the site is worth ten minutes for the way it talks about a mechanical product — quiet, technical, and entirely free of marketing… meta-language.

Standards Fight · AI in the Browser

“Mozilla doesn’t want Chrome shipping a Prompt API.”

Google has been quietly pushing a built-in Prompt API in Chrome — a way for any web page to call a local language model that ships inside the browser. Mozilla’s standards team filed a formal opposition, arguing that bundling an opinionated model behind a standard JavaScript surface puts every other browser vendor in the impossible position of either matching the bundle or being silently unable to run the same web. Five hundred and sixty-four points and the better part of a Saturday’s comment section say HN agrees the question is real.

06 · Weird Science
06

Snowball Earth was hiding a stranger climate underneath.

A new paper revisits the “Snowball Earth” episodes — the deep-time stretches when most of the planet’s surface appears to have been encased in ice — and proposes that the period was not a single, stable freeze but a chaotic, oscillating system. The climate didn’t just go cold; it cycled, on geologic timescales, between hard glaciation and partial thaw, in patterns the existing models don’t cleanly explain. The authors suggest the planet was running a feedback loop that current Earth-system science has only the faintest analog for.

Pure speculative pleasure to read on a Sunday. Nothing here changes what you do tomorrow. But it’s the kind of paper that quietly resets your sense of how settled deep history actually is.

The climate didn’t just go cold. It cycled — on a geological metronome no one has reconstructed.

CS Research · First Principles

You can beat the binary search.

Daniel Lemire, who has spent a career publishing tight, measurable improvements to data-structure folklore, posted a benchmark this weekend showing that a carefully written branchless search routine can outperform a classical binary search on modern hardware — not by a marginal constant factor, but by an amount that should make any library author look up. The trick lies in eliminating mispredicted branches and letting the CPU’s prefetcher do the work the comparison was hiding from it.

Two hundred and twenty-six points, a hundred and ten comments, and a healthy thread of practitioners trying the variant on their own workloads. Binary search has been taught the same way for fifty years. The point of posts like this isn’t that the textbook is wrong — it’s that the hardware underneath the textbook moved, and very few of the algorithms inherited from the 1970s have been re-measured against what a modern core actually does.

Hacker Culture · Reverse Engineering
08

Someone reverse-engineered SimTower.

A developer spent months tearing apart the 1994 cult-classic SimTower — the Maxis-published, OPENBook-engine simulation about running a high-rise that ate untold hours of nineties childhoods. The writeup walks through the file formats, the elevator-scheduling logic, and a small archive of weird design decisions only visible by looking at the original 16-bit binary.

It’s the kind of project that has no commercial reason to exist and reads, for that reason, like a love letter. The internet still occasionally produces these. They are worth bookmarking when they appear.

09

Queues, streams, pub/sub — all in your .db.

Honker is a small library that puts durable queues, streams, pub/sub, and a cron scheduler directly inside a SQLite database file. No broker. No daemon. No Redis sitting in the corner consuming memory you forgot you allocated to it. Your background job system becomes a table; your scheduler becomes a row.

One hundred and fifty-eight points and a comment section full of operators describing the smaller services they’d immediately collapse if this works as advertised. SQLite has been quietly absorbing whole categories of infrastructure for years; queues were one of the last big ones standing.

The Number · Browser Privacy
10

LinkedIn is fingerprinting your browser extensions.

6,278
Browser extensions LinkedIn scans for on every page load

Researchers at 404privacy disassembled LinkedIn’s page-load JavaScript and found that the site probes for the presence of more than six thousand specific browser extensions, encrypts the resulting fingerprint, and ships it back with every request. The extension list reads like a portrait of who you are: ad blockers, password managers, accessibility tools, niche developer add-ons, web-clippers, scrapers.

You can’t opt out from inside their site. You can move to a hardened browser profile, run LinkedIn in a sandboxed container, or simply — as several commenters note — spend less of your life on LinkedIn. All three are reasonable. The piece is the best documentation yet of how casually this kind of fingerprinting now happens, on properties most users still treat as benign.

Applies to youYou almost certainly have ten or more of those 6,278 extensions installed. If LinkedIn is open in a tab right now, it has a high-confidence fingerprint of you that survives logout. A separate browser profile or a container tab handles the worst of it in about ninety seconds.
