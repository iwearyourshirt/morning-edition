The Big One · Web Standards

Mozilla draws a line under Chrome’s in‐browser AI.

01

Mozilla’s standards team filed a formal “harmful” position today on Chrome’s proposed Prompt API — the WebAPI that would let any website call a built-in large language model directly out of the browser. The position document is dense, careful, and uncharacteristically pointed: shipping a non-standardized LLM endpoint as part of the platform, Mozilla argues, ossifies a single vendor’s model as a de facto web primitive. Five hundred and sixty-four upvotes and two hundred and seven comments on HN by mid-morning, almost all of them grappling with the question of who, exactly, gets to decide which model lives behind window.ai.

The fight that begins today will not be settled this year. But the stakes are unusually clean: the browser is the last neutral surface most users have left, and a vendor-supplied AI baked into it is a structurally different thing than an AI you chose to install.

Worth reading in fullIf you build anything on the web — including the kinds of HTML artifacts you generate from Cowork — this is the platform-politics fight that decides what “AI in the browser” means for the next decade. The thread is unusually thoughtful for HN.

The Disclosure Question · Open Source
02

CopyFail was never told to Gentoo.

An OSS-Security mailing-list post made the front page this morning with a single, uncomfortable claim: a vulnerability nicknamed CopyFail — one that researchers say touches a swath of low-level Linux tooling — was disclosed to several major distributions weeks ago, and not to Gentoo. Three hundred and twelve points. Two hundred and forty-seven comments. A long, civil, occasionally raw debate about who counts as a coordinated-disclosure partner once the “major distro” list quietly stopped including the rolling-release independents.

The actual vulnerability is the smaller story. The larger one is that the informal trust network that has historically coordinated Linux security has reached the point where being a serious distribution doesn’t automatically buy you a seat at the table. Whether the omission was political, logistical, or accidental is contested. The structural drift it exposes is not.

“Coordinated disclosure is only coordinated for the people you happen to have on speed dial.”

Heads Up · Keep In Your Toolbox
03 · Data Recovery

A stranger’s old hard drive, rescued with one free tool.

A blogger writing under the name “lost-number” bought a stack of old hard drives at an estate sale, ran PhotoRec against them, and walked away with thirty years of someone’s family photographs that had been deleted, formatted-over, and presumed gone. The post is brief, the prose understated, and the gut-punch arrives at the end when the author tracks down the family and offers to mail the drive back.

The technical takeaway is the part to file away in muscle memory: PhotoRec is free, open-source, and astonishingly capable. It walks raw blocks of a disk image, ignores the filesystem entirely, and reassembles anything whose file signature it recognizes. If you have ever lost a file you assumed was gone, the answer is probably not.

Applies to youThe next time a drive misbehaves — yours or someone you’re helping — brew install testdisk first, panic second. PhotoRec ships with TestDisk and recovers from a disk image, so the “don’t write to the drive” rule is easy to follow. Worth the ten minutes it takes to confirm you know the workflow before you need it.

Dev Tools · The Whole Backend In One File

A durable queue, a pub/sub bus, and a cron scheduler — living inside your SQLite file.

Honker.dev launched this morning to a hundred and fifty-eight upvotes and forty-nine generally enthusiastic comments. The pitch: take the patterns most apps reach for a Redis, a message bus, and a cron daemon to satisfy — durable queues, streams, pub/sub, scheduled tasks — and implement them as a thin layer on top of a single SQLite database file. No extra processes. No network hop. One file you can copy, version-control, or back up the same way you back up everything else.

The category of “move the backend into one file” is now well past novelty and into the working-tool phase. Honker is the latest, and probably not the last, datapoint that infrastructure complexity has been mostly accidental.

The Long Essay · Construction Physics

“How an oil refinery works.”

Brian Potter at Construction Physics has done the rare thing: written a clear, patient, end-to-end explanation of what is actually happening inside an oil refinery — from the crude unloading dock through the distillation tower, the cracking units, the reformer, the hydrotreater, and out the loading rack as gasoline, jet fuel, and the long tail of petrochemical feedstocks. Two hundred and ninety points and eighty-three comments largely composed of refinery engineers correcting each other politely in public. Weird Science · Paleoclimate

Snowball Earth may have hidden a stranger climate cycle.

A new paper summarized on Science X argues that the long-accepted “Snowball Earth” episodes — the stretches of Cryogenian time, around 700 million years ago, when ice may have advanced all the way to the equator — were not simple deep-freezes but the visible peaks of a much more elaborate climate oscillation. The proposed cycle involves carbon dioxide accumulation under ice cover, intermittent partial melt events triggered by volcanic emissions, and a feedback loop with ocean chemistry that the textbook story has been too coarse to capture.

The paper is careful, the press release less so. What is suggestive is that the new model accounts for several anomalies in the geochemical record that the textbook model has had to wave away for two decades. Worth flagging as the kind of paper that might quietly rewrite an introductory chapter in another five years.

08

Every US Government auction, finally searchable in one place.

An indie developer named Sam aggregated twenty-eight separate federal auction sites — GSA, the US Marshals, the Treasury, surplus seizures, vehicle disposals, the strange ones too — into a single search interface called BidProwl. Two hundred and nineteen points and sixty-seven comments, most of them some variation of “I cannot believe nobody has done this before.” The federal-auction ecosystem has been a labyrinth of bad PHP for thirty years, and a single side-project just collapsed the whole thing into one box and a sort order.

Dev · Quietly Powerful
09

Full-text search, in DuckDB, in one file.

Peter Doherty’s walkthrough of DuckDB’s native full-text-search extension showed up this morning, and it’s the cleanest demonstration yet of what the single-file analytical database has quietly become: a Postgres-grade query engine that fits in a binary smaller than VS Code’s changelog, with FTS, JSON, vector search, and HTTP file reads all attached without a network hop.

The tutorial walks through indexing a corpus, ranking with BM25, and combining text matches with structured columns in a single SQL statement. If you have ever stood up an Elasticsearch cluster for a project that would have fit comfortably in a single SQLite file, this is the post that will retroactively annoy you.

Applies to youFor anything you’ve been considering throwing Algolia or Elasticsearch at — an archive of your own writing, a private knowledge base, a search box on a static site — DuckDB now does it inside one binary with no daemon. The kind of capability worth knowing exists before you need it.

Closing Number · Home Networking

Ten-gigabit Ethernet, retrofitted through a Victorian terrace.

10×
Speedup over standard Gigabit, achieved with about a weekend’s patience

Giles Thomas published a long, beautifully unglamorous post documenting exactly what it took to get a working 10 Gb/s Ethernet link running through the walls of an old British house: the SFP+ transceivers he tried and rejected, the heat issues with consumer cards, the firmware bug that cost him an afternoon, the moment everything finally negotiated at the full link speed. A hundred and twenty-four points, ninety-three comments, the warmest possible HN tone.

The reason a post like this hits the front page in 2026 is not the throughput. It is the way the post is written: patiently, in plain language, with every dead end recorded. The internet still has rooms in it where the goal is to leave behind a record of what worked, in case it helps the next person.
