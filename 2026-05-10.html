Claude Code throws a fit over the word “OpenClaw.”

01

Eight hundred and sixty-five upvotes and nearly five hundred comments deep, the front page is fixated on a single bizarre claim: that Claude Code will refuse certain requests — or quietly bill at higher rates — if your git commits mention a fictional competitor called “OpenClaw.” The thread is half forensic, half theater, with developers running their own diff tests and posting receipts.

Whether the behavior is intentional, an artifact of training data, or something stranger, the discourse around it has already accomplished what most product launches can’t: it made a coding assistant feel, briefly, like it had a personality. And not necessarily a flattering one.

Directly For You · Anthropic
02

Opus 4.7 already knows the real Kelsey.

An essayist at The Argument sat down to use Opus 4.7 anonymously and discovered, several turns in, that it had quietly stitched together enough public crumbs to identify her by name. Not from a leaked profile, not from a logged-in session — just from the texture of how she wrote.

The piece isn’t a panic. It’s an honest reckoning with what “anonymous” means once a model has read most of the internet about you. Whether you find that liberating or unnerving depends on what you’ve published. Either way, the bar for plausible deniability against a frontier model just dropped through the floor.

“The model didn’t know me because someone told it. It knew me because I did.”
You should read this todayYou publish under your own name across multiple surfaces. The essay is short, sharp, and a useful mental model for the next time you start a fresh chat thinking the slate is clean.

03 · Supply Chain

Shai-Hulud-themed malware found inside PyTorch Lightning.

Semgrep’s research team caught a malicious dependency riding into the PyTorch Lightning AI training library — named after the giant sandworms of Dune and apparently authored by someone with a sense of literary flourish to match their threat model. Two hundred and ninety-nine points and ninety-seven comments on HN, almost all from people audibly checking their lockfiles in real time.

The mechanism is dispiritingly familiar by now: a popular package’s transitive dependency gets compromised, the malicious version sits quietly in your pip install, and your training run doubles as a reverse shell. What makes this one notable isn’t novelty — it’s that PyTorch Lightning is mainstream enough that nobody outside the security crowd was watching.

Applies to you if……you, or anything you’ve installed, pulls PyTorch Lightning into a virtualenv. Pin versions, audit your recent installs, and if you train models, treat this week’s lockfile as suspect until Semgrep’s remediation guidance lands.

AI · Web Standards

Mozilla draws a line against Chrome’s in-browser Prompt API.

Five hundred and sixty-four upvotes and the better part of a Saturday afternoon were spent on Mozilla’s formal opposition to Chrome shipping a built-in Prompt API — the proposed JavaScript hook that would let any web page call a local model for inference. Mozilla’s objection is unusually principled: this isn’t a standard, it’s a Google product surface masquerading as one.

The fight is the most consequential web-platform argument of the year. If the Prompt API ships unilaterally in Chrome, every site you visit gets a model on tap, and the question of which model becomes the new browser war — fought entirely inside the platform you don’t see.

Long Read · Surveillance History

“The whistleblower who walked into Room 641A.”

An MIT Press book excerpt revisits how Mark Klein, a quiet AT&T technician in San Francisco, found a locked room full of fiber splitters siphoning the entire backbone to the NSA — and how he eventually convinced the EFF to take him seriously. Three hundred and seventy-nine points, an unusually warm comment thread for a Sunday morning. The piece is part biography, part procedural, and a reminder that the surveillance architecture most of us now treat as ambient was, twenty years ago, a single closet in a single building that one man decided to talk about.

06 · Privacy · Actionable
06

Rivian quietly added a kill switch for everything.

Three hundred and thirty-one upvotes for a single support page: Rivian now lets owners disable all data collection from their vehicles — cellular, telematics, the entire fleet feedback pipeline — through a setting in the app. Toggle it off and the truck stops phoning home. The HN crowd is, predictably, both delighted and waiting for the asterisk.

The interesting part isn’t the toggle itself. It’s that an automaker built and shipped one. For a decade the industry consensus has been that opt-outs were a regulatory threat to be managed, not a feature to be marketed. Rivian just changed the spread.

An automaker shipped an off switch. That hasn’t been the spread for a decade.

Hacker Culture · Field Notes

A Game Boy emulator, written in F#, written down.

Nick Kossolapov spent some unspecified but clearly substantial portion of his life writing a Game Boy emulator in F# — and then, in the older and rarer tradition, wrote down how he did it. The resulting essay is long, methodical, and deeply pleasurable in the way that only a well-documented systems project can be. One hundred and seventy-six points and forty-three comments, most of them other people who have already started cloning the repo.

F# is an unusual choice for emulation, and that’s the point. The post is less “here is how the Game Boy works” and more “here is what happens when you bring a language designed for correctness to a problem usually solved with C and prayer.” Required reading for anyone who suspects the next great wave of hobby projects might come from people taking unusual tools seriously.

Jeff Bridges has built you a panoramic film camera.

The actor — long a serious film photographer — just unveiled the Wideluxx, a fully mechanical, swing-lens panoramic camera designed as a spiritual successor to the discontinued Widelux. No batteries. No firmware. Just a slow, satisfying mechanical arc across 35mm film. Forty-six upvotes, sixteen comments, and a small chorus of professional photographers who were apparently waiting for exactly this.

It’s an odd, beautiful object that quietly insists the analog corner of photography isn’t a museum exhibit yet. If you’ve been looking for a creative side quest with a high cost of entry and no notifications, here it is.

Honker puts queues, pub/sub, and cron inside SQLite.

Honker.dev launched today with a wonderfully economical pitch: durable queues, streams, pub/sub, and a cron scheduler — all living inside a single SQLite file you already know how to back up. One hundred and fifty-eight points, forty-nine mostly impressed comments, and a small subgenre of replies pointing out that this is the platonic shape every weekend project secretly wants.

The category of “the whole backend is one file” keeps producing surprisingly capable software. If you’ve ever stood up Redis just to schedule three things, give Honker a long look before reaching for infrastructure.

LinkedIn is checking your browser for 6,278 specific extensions.

6,278
Browser extensions LinkedIn fingerprints on every request

404Privacy published a teardown today showing that LinkedIn’s web client probes for the presence of more than six thousand named browser extensions on every page load — then encrypts the resulting fingerprint and ships it back with each request. The post landed two hundred and ninety-nine upvotes and a hundred and twenty-one mostly stunned comments before lunch.

The list itself is the story. Ad blockers, privacy tools, scrapers, recruiter automations — LinkedIn has built a near-complete catalog of the things their users are doing to avoid being seen the way LinkedIn wants to see them. The encryption is the giveaway: this isn’t telemetry, it’s reconnaissance.

Applies to you if……you keep a LinkedIn tab open. Worth reading the 404Privacy post in full and deciding whether you’re comfortable with what your browser is broadcasting back to them every time you scroll the feed.
