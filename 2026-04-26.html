The Big One · AI & Mathematics

An amateur, a chatbot, and a sixty‑year‑old Erdős problem.

01

Scientific American reports that a hobbyist mathematician, working with ChatGPT as a sparring partner, has produced a result on a problem Paul Erdős posed decades ago and that working number theorists had circled for sixty years without closing. Two hundred and forty‑one points on Hacker News by mid‑morning, with a hundred and fifty‑five comments oscillating between cautious admiration and the predictable “but who actually did the math?”

The honest answer is: both of them, in a way that doesn’t fit any of the existing labels. The chatbot didn’t prove the theorem. The amateur didn’t do it alone. What the pair found together — one offering vibes, one offering pattern‑matching at scale — is the first wide‑angle look at what amateur math might be in the second half of this decade.

Directly For You · Coding Assistants

It’s OK to use coding assistants to revive the projects you were never going to finish.

Matthew Brunelle’s short essay, which the Hacker News front page took to immediately — two hundred and forty‑six points, a hundred and thirty‑five comments — is a piece of permission slip writing for a particular kind of guilt. The half‑built side projects in your folder. The shell scripts that would save twenty minutes a week if you ever got around to them. The little tools you sketched on a napkin and then never reopened. Brunelle’s argument: the moral panic about AI‑assisted coding has obscured a simple fact — for the personal projects you were never going to ship anyway, the alternative isn’t purer code, it’s no code.

The comment section, predictably, splits along familiar lines. The interesting half is the one quietly admitting that, yes, this is exactly what they did over the weekend, and the project finally exists.

“The alternative to AI‑assisted code, for projects you would never have finished, is not better code. It is no code.”
Applies to youYou already run skill‑based Cowork workflows and a scheduled daily magazine pipeline. Brunelle is describing the exact muscle you’ve been building. Worth thirty seconds of acknowledgment that the half‑finished projects in your folder are now finishable in a way they weren’t three years ago.

Heads Up · Check Your Phone
03 · iOS Weirdness

An app is silently re‑installing itself on someone’s iPhone every day.

A Hacker News user filed an Ask HN this morning describing exactly the sort of thing that is supposed to be impossible on iOS: an app they uninstall keeps coming back, day after day, without their consent and without any obvious mechanism. A hundred and thirty‑eight points and seventy comments later, the thread has become a small forensics seminar — iCloud sync, Family Sharing artifacts, MDM profiles, App Store automatic re‑downloads, and at least one theory involving a forgotten configuration profile from years ago.

No conclusive answer yet. Which is, in some ways, the story. Apple’s walled garden is supposed to make this class of problem unthinkable. The fact that a competent user can’t even narrow down the cause is its own quiet indictment of how opaque the platform has become.

Applies to you if……you carry an iPhone. Settings → App Store → check “App Downloads” and “Automatic Downloads.” Then Settings → General → VPN & Device Management — look for any configuration profiles you don’t recognize. Family Sharing → Purchase Sharing is the third place to look. Five‑minute audit, worth doing.

Sharp Essay · Photography

“Flickr was the first and last great photo platform.”

PetaPixel’s essay is the kind of measured, slightly elegiac writing the medium deserves more of. Flickr arrived early, got the social mechanics right almost by accident, was bought, mismanaged, sold, and finally landed in caretaker hands at SmugMug — and somehow, two decades on, is still the only place online where serious photographers actually talk to each other in public about photographs as photographs. The author’s claim is that nothing has replaced it because nothing of the architecture that made it possible exists anymore. Read it for the framing, not the obituary.

06 · AI & Privacy
06

OpenAI ships a privacy filter, and the question is whether you trust the audit.

OpenAI announced its Privacy Filter today — a feature meant to scrub personally identifying information out of inputs before they hit the model, with a configurable allow‑list for the categories you don’t mind sending. A hundred and seventy points, twenty‑nine comments, with the predictable mix of cautious approval and pointed reminders that “privacy filter” is one of those phrases whose meaning depends entirely on whether you can verify what the filter actually did.

The mechanism is sound. The marketing pitches it as protection. The substance is a useful affordance for teams who already trust OpenAI — and a different conversation entirely for everyone else. The interesting bit is whether other model providers ship similar primitives and how the contracts evolve.

A privacy filter is exactly as private as the company building the filter is willing to be audited.

AI Research · Open Models

DeepSeek‑V4, day zero, with verified RL.

The LMSYS team published a same‑day technical write‑up of DeepSeek‑V4: fast inference through SGLang, plus a verified reinforcement‑learning pipeline they’re calling Miles. Day‑zero support is the kind of thing that used to take weeks; the fact that an open model now arrives with a battle‑tested serving stack and a reproducible RL recipe on the same morning is itself the news.

The benchmarks will be argued about for a week. The architectural pattern — large open release, immediate community tooling, transparent post‑training pipeline — is the durable signal. Each of these releases makes the next one easier. The closed labs are still ahead. The gap is narrower than it was six months ago, and the cadence is the part that matters.

Crypto · Mainline
09

GnuPG: post‑quantum lands in master.

The GnuPG announcement list confirms it: post‑quantum key types are now in mainline GnuPG. Sixteen points and a single comment as of this morning, which is roughly the inverse of how important this actually is. PGP isn’t having a renaissance; it doesn’t need one. It quietly underwrites release signing for half the open‑source software ecosystem, and the day its keys can be retroactively broken by a quantum computer is the day a great many supply chains have a problem they cannot patch their way out of.

This is the patch. It does not have to be exciting to be load‑bearing.
