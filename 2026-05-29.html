Opus 4.8 lands.

01

Sharp Essay · AI Critique
02

Various LLM smells.

A working developer published a careful, slightly weary taxonomy of the small tells — ‘smells’ in the code-review sense — that mark a file as LLM-touched. Over-explanatory comments that paraphrase the function name. Suspicious unanimity in variable spelling. Defensive null checks where the language already guarantees the invariant. A dozen of them, catalogued with the eye of someone who clearly uses these tools every day.

The piece isn’t anti-AI. It’s the opposite: a reader mapping the signature so they can spot it, refine it, and ship cleaner work. Two hundred and twenty-nine upvotes and a hundred and sixty-six comments from an audience that recognized themselves on both sides of the diff.

“The smell isn’t that the model wrote it. The smell is that nobody read it before committing.”

Heads Up · Platform Risk
03 · Where Your Work Lives

GitHub bans a researcher. The chilling effect lands.

A well-known Windows security researcher had their GitHub account terminated this week after publishing proof-of-concept exploits for two unpatched Windows zero-days. Microsoft owns GitHub. Microsoft also owns Windows. The researcher argues, with some force, that those two facts are now operationally indistinguishable, and has promised further retaliation.

The deeper question isn’t whether Microsoft was right to act — reasonable people disagree on what responsible disclosure ought to look like. The deeper question is whether the infrastructure that most of open source now sits on top of can be relied upon to host work that is adversarial to its parent company. The comment thread reads like a town square watching a precedent set.

Applies to you if……any of your projects, skills repos, or magazine archive live exclusively on GitHub. Mirror what matters. A self-hosted Forgejo instance or a private Codeberg account takes an afternoon to set up and removes a single point of platform risk.

Builder Story · Hardware

A dorm-room keyboard part, quietly past a million.

Nick Winans designed the Nice Nano — a tiny wireless microcontroller board built for hand-soldered mechanical keyboards — while he was still in college. He wrote the build log this week, eight years later, after the board crossed a million dollars in lifetime revenue. The essay is the rare specimen that’s interesting on both sides: as hardware war story, and as a quiet rebuke to the idea that small, niche, deeply-loved products aren’t a real business.

The mechanics are the lesson. He didn’t pivot. He didn’t raise. He shipped a board for an obsessive hobbyist community, kept it cheap, and let compounding do the work. The Nice Nano is now the default microcontroller for an entire category of custom keyboards.

06

Whole-house Wi-Fi roaming, built from OpenWRT and patience.

Rui Carmo documented his weekend project: convincing three OpenWRT access points to behave as a single roaming Wi-Fi network without buying into a closed mesh ecosystem. The post hits exactly the right level of detail — enough that you could replicate it, terse enough that you won’t get lost. The result is the kind of network the consumer brands charge five hundred dollars to deliver, assembled from hardware that costs a fraction of that.

The deeper point is that the gap between ‘consumer mesh router’ and ‘OpenWRT with 802.11r enabled’ is mostly a UX gap. The hard engineering work has been done, in the open, for years.

What the big brands are really selling isn’t the protocol. It’s the wizard that sets it up for you.

Languages · Worth Reading

Coalton: an ML-flavored Lisp, in earnest.

Coalton is a statically typed Lisp embedded in Common Lisp, borrowing the algebraic types and rigor of Haskell and OCaml while sitting inside the live, mutable, image-based world of CL. The pitch sounds like a contradiction in terms — ‘a typed Lisp’ — and the implementation is more honest than the average academic side project. You can call into it from existing Common Lisp code. The type errors are real type errors.

This is a small project with a small audience and an outsized share of careful thought behind it. That ratio is usually the early signal that a tool has staying power. Ninety-four upvotes and fourteen comments, all serious. Worth bookmarking even if you don’t write a line of Lisp this year.

Dev · Backend Architecture
09

Durable workflows on postgres.

DBOS published a clean technical brief making the case that you don’t need a separate queue, a separate workflow engine, and a separate state store to run durable, fault-tolerant workflows. You need postgres. The whole argument lands in a single observation: Postgres already provides transactions, persistence, and atomicity — the three primitives every workflow engine ends up reimplementing badly. Wire those into a runtime, and the rest is a thin layer.

Two hundred and seventy-two upvotes, a hundred and fifteen comments. Most of them are either ‘finally’ or ‘yes, but Temporal does X.’ Both are partly right. The piece is worth reading before you reach for a heavier stack on your next side project.
